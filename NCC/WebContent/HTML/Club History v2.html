  • Club History

    Newbury Cricket Club is said to have been founded in 1822, although where this information originally came from is no longer known. There was certainly a Newbury Club in 1850, and records exist of meetings held at the White Hart Hotel in 1869 to formalise the arrangements by which pitches were to be prepared “on the meadow adjoining Victoria Park.” In those days Country House cricket was a social phenomenon, and many of Newbury’s fixtures were against the great Estates of the day – places like Highclere, Aldermaston Court, Ramsbury and Arlington Grange. One of this era’s leading players was J. Beauchamp who had played for Surrey, and was landlord of the local Cricketers Inn. In 1876 he made 128 against Basingstoke, in a total of 371, after which the opposition was bowled out for fifty. W.G. Grace is recorded as having played in a match against Newbury in the same year, and the club also played and beat a United South of England side under the captaincy of the great opera writer W.S. Gilbert, whose team included Jupp, Charlwood and Lillywhite – all famous cricketers of the time.

    Cricket was popular enough, and of a high enough standard for the club to charge admission to matches, and in 1881this in part funded the erection of a picturesque thatched pavilion adjacent to the ground in Victoria Park, which can be seen in photographs of the period.

    Despite this success, in the later 1880s the club’s fortunes took a downward turn. Another local club, Northbrook CC came into existence, attracting a lot of members, so that by 1890 the old club found itself in financial difficulty. However in 1891 it seems local cricketers must have decided to join forces, as both clubs were dissolved and a reconstituted Newbury Cricket Club emerged, still based in Victoria Park, with Ernest Slocock as secretary and his brother Charles becoming club captain. J. Beauchamp had by this time retired as a player but had become club umpire, so there was obviously some continuity between the organisations. This merger heralded the start of what was possibly the strongest period in the club’s history. In 1898 matches were hosted against the full Berkshire side, (drawn) and won against an M.C.C. side that included four professionals. Newbury’s grounds man Bill Cave was also their star player that year, scoring 1,042 runs including three centuries at an average of 37 and taking 163 wickets at only 8 runs apiece. Maybe he knew how to produce the kind of pitches which suited him.

    The pitches must have been good however, because the quality of the Town Ground is mentioned in several documents, most notably a booklet entitled “Newbury in the Victorian Era” by F.H.Stillman. It was this however which lead to the next hiccup in the history of the club, because the rise in popularity of football in the early 20th century lead to the joint use of the ground. Newbury Town F.C. was fast becoming a leading light in the Berks and Bucks football association, and the constant pressure on the ground during the football season made it increasingly difficult to prepare the class of wickets for which Beachamp and Cave had become known. With the deterioration of the pitches, the class of cricket played also suffered

    Worse was to follow when at the end of the 1909 season a fire destroyed the pavilion, taking with it most of the club’s equipment. Another, more modern building was erected, but within only a few years the First World War brought this era to a close. The first class County Championship was abandoned, partly because public opinion was against its continuation in wartime, and also because many leading players were fighting in Germany.

    Presumably the same factors applied to club cricket, although we do not have records of how these decisions were made.

    Newbury Cricket Club would like to thank the Newbury Weekly News for permission to use information and extracts from articles written by John Taylor, which were originally published between 1952 and 1987, and for various photographs originally printed in the newspaper.

  • In 1921 a meeting was held at the Guildhall to revive the club under the captaincy of C. Thirkhill. A young spin bowler by the name of Bill Charman was just starting to make his name in the area, and in his first senior match, aged sixteen he took 8 for 15. Pitches at the old town ground had continued to deteriorate, and at some point in the twenties the club began to play away matches only, Victoria Park finally losing its status as the headquarters of cricket in Newbury. At the same time however, on a small ground off Boundary Road in Stroud Green a new club called Westwood CC began to play matches in the newly founded Newbury District Cricket League.

    Without a home ground even for practice, Newbury CC held no attractions for young cricketers, and many of their members, including Charman joined Westwood, and another small local club called Newbury Great Western, who played on a ground off the Bath Road called “The Lawns.” Neither of these new clubs had aspirations beyond what was purely village cricket, but in 1929 the original Newbury CC was again dissolved, and with the new influx of players Westwood grew to take on the responsibility of being Newbury’s representative club. The strength of fixtures improved, soon including Swindon, Reading, Andover and Wokingham.

    In 1938, a large section of derelict land to the west of Northbrook Street, which up until then had been used as a rubbish dump, was identified by the Council as having potential for redevelopment as a sports ground. Plans were drawn up to create a new home for cricket in Newbury, and Westwood agreed that if they were given the use of this new ground at Northcroft, they would change their name to Newbury Cricket Club. The Club Captain at that time, A.D. Sutton, was a great advocate of the revival of the town club. In 1939 Westwood’s committee, which now included Bill Charman, also reached agreement with Great Western for the two clubs to merge, but before this could be put into effect, war once more put a stop to organised club cricket in England. Bill Charman had taken 710 wickets at an average of 9.03, including three hat-tricks, one of which was four in four balls. He had also made 4,907 runs at an average of 21.91.

  • During the early 1940s, work was started on the levelling of the ground at Northcroft, and On 1st November 1945 the Mayor of Newbury, Mr F.D.Bazett MC invited a new incarnation of Newbury Cricket Club to put together a fixture list for the summer of 1946 at Northcroft. Sutton and Charman enlisted the help of the enthusiastic and capable George Lloyd as Secretary and on 4th May 1946 Sutton captained the first team ever to play cricket at Northcrcoft in a match against The Nautical College Pangbourne. Sid Inns (Honorary Secretary throughout the seventies and eighties) and Eric Longshaw (Fixture Secretary for many years and Club President to this day,) were also amongst the founder members. Not many clubs had yet re-formed, and there were plenty of players available. George Lloyd was able to put together two Saturday sides and a midweek team every week. He was also a successful fund raiser, and in 1947 a new pavilion was built, complete with a canteen, changing rooms and showers.

    Bill Charman retired in 1951, but his son Peter also played for the club for a while, in 1952 taking 7 for 0 against Southampton Stoats, who before his spell had been 110 for 2. He hit the stumps five times and was given two LBW decisions. Peter later told the Newbury Weekly News that his fondest memory was as a youngster bowling against Swindon, with his dad at the other end when they took all ten wickets between them. Peter’s time at the club was shortlived however – he later went on to play for Newtown CC at Horris Hill School, said to have been one of the finest grounds of the day, and joined Falkland when Newtown disbanded in 1973. Surely one of the most successful father and son bowling partnerships in local club cricket.

    A.D. Sutton also retired in 1951, having partnered George Lloyd in forming the solid foundations of a club which would continue to play cricket at Northcroft up to the present day. He was succeeded by Malcolm Fraser, five years previously the Newbury Grammar School Captain, who started his Newbury CC career with a century against RAF Yatesbury. Another golden period at Newbury was beginning, with J.A. Mence, L.W. Ray, G.W.S. Waites and C.L.H.Cox all representing Berkshire. A minor Counties match was played at Northcroft, and in a two day game at the School Ground in Fifth Road a Newbury XI captained by E.B.Lloyd, who scored 67, beat Dorset by 8 wickets.

    Not everything was easy for the club however. It was recorded in 1952 that some townsmen, who in theory still had traditional rights to graze cattle and horses on the land, had been surreptitiously leaving the gates to the ground open in order to turn out their livestock on the outfield overnight. It is also on record that in 1955 Newbury 1st XI scored their lowest ever total – 19 all out at Wokingham.
